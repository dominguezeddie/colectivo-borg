# Community — Borg Collective

## Beyond Code

The Borg Collective is not just a software project.
It is a permanent social and community infrastructure.

If the Borg stayed only in the niche of those who know how to program
or configure antennas, it would lose the other 90% of the community
that shares the conviction but whose trench is elsewhere.

---

## The Three Channels of Contribution

Just as machines have three roles (Executor, Aggregator, Validator),
people can also contribute from three different channels.
You don't need to know how to program to be part of the Collective.

### 💪 Technical Muscle
*For those who know code or hardware*

- Programmers improving the core, protocol, or modules
- WISP technicians setting up seed nodes in the territory
- People who recover old computers, install the system,
  and donate them to schools or community centers
- Translators expanding the Borg to new languages

### 💰 Economic Fuel
*For those who want to support without programming*

- People who don't know what a Python socket is, but understand
  that AI monopolies are dangerous
- Their contribution — whether the equivalent of a coffee per month
  or cooperative donations — will not enrich any shareholder
- Goes directly to: community hardware, seed node bandwidth,
  pendrives to distribute the system in rural schools

> *"I don't understand code, but this month I contributed the equivalent*
> *of two liters of gasoline so that the Borg node at the community*
> *library stays on."*
>
> That is community technological sovereignty.

### 🌍 Territorial Support
*For those working in the social field*

- Rural teachers and school educators
- Social activists and community workers
- Social work students
- Regional translators and speakers of local languages
- Anyone who can test the system in the field,
  expand local vocabularies, and audit that the Borg's responses
  are useful for the 60% left behind

## Why the Foundation Model is Correct

**Shield against corporate acquisition**
Being a public benefit foundation with AGPL v3 license,
no multinational can buy the Borg and close it.
The foundation cannot be sold. It is born from the community and dies in the community.

**Radical cash transparency**
Like Wikipedia: every peso that comes in is publicly disclosed.
*"We had X in revenue: 70% on community node hosting,
20% on pendrives for rural schools, 10% on administration."*

**Institutional bargaining power**
As an individual, talking to universities or municipalities
to give up space on their towers is an uphill battle.
As the **Borg Collective Foundation**, the project stands
as a legitimate institutional actor of the popular economy
and free software.

---

## What the Foundation Can Do That No Corporation Will

A foundation with territorial presence can do very concrete things
that no corporate software project can or wants to do —
because they are not interested and lack the structure to do them.

- **Field hardware verification:** someone goes to the rural school,
  sees what they have, evaluates if it can run the Borg, installs it,
  leaves it running, and follows up. No app store does that.

- **Bulk pendrive purchasing:** with collective funds, 500 pendrives
  with the system already recorded are bought and distributed where most
  needed — not where there is the most market.

- **Network of territorial technicians:** the author's WISP experience
  already implies knowing that network — the technicians who install
  antennas in rural areas, who know where there is connectivity and where
  there isn't. That human network already exists. The foundation gives
  it structure and resources.

- **Real priority criteria:** a resource-poor rural school has priority
  over an urban office. That can only be decided by an institution with
  explicit values — not a market algorithm.

The foundation converts donations into visible, traceable hardware.
Someone donates the equivalent of two pendrives, and can see in the annual
report that those two pendrives are in the San Rafael library functioning
as nodes. That builds real trust. And trust brings more donations.

## Global Redistribution: From North to South

A foundation with branches in different countries can raise funds where
there is more economic capacity and redistribute where there is more real
need. Resources come from Germany, Sweden, or Canada and are redistributed
to rural Africa, Asia, or Latin America. Money does not stay where it is
generated — it goes where it is needed.

This has a name in the NGO world: South-South flow mediated by the North.
Wikipedia works like this. It raises funds mainly in rich countries and
serves mainly poor countries. The Borg adds a dimension that Wikipedia lacks:
physical hardware.

When the German branch of the Borg Foundation buys 10,000 pendrives and sends
them to Africa, that is not charity — it is infrastructure. Charity creates
dependency. Infrastructure creates capacity. Those 10,000 pendrives do not
give Africa access to a corporate AI that can be cut off tomorrow. They give
them their own nodes, their own cryptographic identity, their own reputation
within the Collective. That cannot be taken away.

## The Community Mobile Node: The Old Cell Phone as Infrastructure

In Africa and Asia, the old cell phone is much more common than an old PC.
There is no library PC — there is a 2018 Android in almost everyone's pocket.
The foundation can receive donations of old cell phones, record the system
on them, and distribute them as community mobile nodes — not as personal
phones but as shared infrastructure.

The concept is the mobile equivalent of Borg Dedicated:

- The cell phone starts up.
- No apps, no social networks, nothing.
- Only the Borg Terminal and the internet connection appear.
- The cell phone ceases to be a personal device and becomes a community node.

Technically implemented with Android kiosk mode, custom launcher that replaces
the home screen with the Borg Terminal, or minimal ROM with only Python and
the Borg core for very old hardware.

A rural school in Tanzania receives 20 old cell phones donated from Germany,
all with the Borg recorded, connected to the school's WiFi. That is a cluster
of 20 nodes for the cost of shipping. The old cell phone that in Europe or
Buenos Aires goes to the electronic waste bin, in the hands of the Borg
Foundation, becomes community cognitive infrastructure for a school in
Mozambique or Haiti. That does not exist anywhere today.

## Lighthouse Nodes

A **Lighthouse Node** is a permanent, stable, public node sustained by the
community — not by an individual.

It can live in:
- A community library
- A rural school
- An electric cooperative
- A neighborhood association office
- A public university

### Local critical mass strategy

The Collective does not need 10,000 global nodes to be useful. It needs
functional local clusters.

One Lighthouse Node in a rural school in San Luis, another in a library in
Mendoza, and another in a cooperative in Chilecito — those three nodes already
generate value for those communities. The network grows by territorial
annexation, not by anonymous planetary accumulation.

Critical mass is not a magic number. It is the number of nodes needed for a
user to get a useful response faster than they would without the network.
In a small but reliable network, that can be as few as 5 nodes.

If your organization wants to host a Lighthouse Node,
open an Issue with the title: `[LIGHTHOUSE NODE] Institution name — City, Country`

## The First Node Is Not Alone: The Value of Being a Pioneer

The first node dilemma — "why connect to an empty network?" —
is solved with three pillars that deliver immediate value from Day Zero:

**1. The Borg as a local cognitive bunker:** CB-001 organizes thinking in RAM,
Write-Back persistence protects memory with physical privacy, and the preloaded
knowledge base (manuals, WISP guides, public domain texts) turns the pendrive
into a portable library without internet.

**2. Pioneer reputation mining:** the first nodes receive a multiplier on their
Contribution Score (x2, x3) and accumulate absolute priority for when the
network scales. Being a pioneer is not an act of faith — it is an investment
with guaranteed return in future priority.

**3. The good parasite:** if the Collective is still empty, the local node
connects to Wikipedia, open source repositories, and public digital libraries,
delivering information to the user free of commercial tracking and advertising.
The Borg "does things" from day one, even though underneath it uses borrowed
pipes while building its own.

> *"The first node does not connect because it is promised a splendid future.*
> *It connects because the individual Borg already serves it today."*
